- Hawai’i Feb 22, 2001 : Down Kilueau by Bicycle -

Went to Volcano National Park yesterday. Stopped off at Punalu'u, home of the black (like ace'o'spades black) sand beach and giant green sea turtles.

Got to ride my bike around Kilueau volcano yesterday with son Peter. Son Ben and Wife Nancy wandered around the park while Peter and I took the crater rim road (~12 miles) ride . That took us about 2 hours because we stopped to rubberneck.

When we finished, Peter and I were so pumped that we petitioned Mdm. Pele's local representative to let us ride our bikes down the volcano to Pahala on Hwy 11.

She said, no.

We demonstrated superior intellect by appealing to coolness factor of being able to cruise 20 miles down a nearly continuous grade on bicycles from 4500 ft elevation to somewhere around 1000ft.

She said, no.

We tried employing emotional blackmail with sad, whiny faces, but oxygen depravation and prospect of awesome ride had us giggling like hyenas on Nitrous Oxide.

She said, no.

We whined and bitched for another 20 minutes.

She said, to either get in the car or ride back to Kailua-Kona on our own.

So, we did the manly thing and took off down the road and told her to meet us at Pahala. When we got to the Hwy 11 the road sign said that  Kailua was 96 miles *that* way, and Hilo 28 miles that other way. The road to Hilo was all downhill. We debated briefly, and agreed she probably wouldn't leave us stranded, because she would have to explain to Mother-in-Law Pat how she left her husband and middle son stranded in the Kau desert of Hawai'i with nothing to their names but two bicycles, 2 bottles of water, light clothing, and wallet full of credit cards. The latter does not come in particularly useful if you do not happen top near any credit card validating machinery.

Peter and I cruised SSW down Hwy 11, debating if we would see wife and mother at Pahala, or maybe Punalu'u down the road. Our map wasn't very good, so I was guessing that we would find Pahala around mile marker 49. For you “haole” (whitey), you should know  that the "Big Island" is actually sparsely populated, and that a town can consist of a MacNut outlet, a coffee mill, and what was once a general store in 1940, before the Nisei were relocated to the camps. The other problem with Hawaiian maps, is that towns are occasionally covered in lava, and cease to exist as anything other than a field of  a'a (crumbly) or pahoehoe (slick, crusty) basalt. Think parking lot after being worked over by backhoes or really big, vicious gophers with steel teeth.

Mile marker 49 came and went with no sign of Pahala. Road ahead looked like it proceeded to the vertical in the wrong direction. This turned out to be an optical illusion, as we cruised over the crest of a small hill and through a perfumed forest for two miles. At Pahala, circa mile marker 51, we were not greeted with lei's as is the island custom, but we did have a very quiet ride back to Kailua down around the southern point, and back up through the Ka'u and South Kona districts.

From Hale Kai O'Kona, 9by9

Michael Mohrbacher
